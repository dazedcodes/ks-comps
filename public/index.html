Now that you're comfortable with the system, and we have no more to say, let's share a fun anecdote.

In most Software Development projects, "debugging" is a common task.

"Bugs" are the little, annoying problems that aren't too hard to fix, but always multiply furiously.

The term "debugging" is popularly attributed to U.S. Navy Rear Admiral Grace Hopper.

Admiral Hopper was working in Harvard University on a Mark II computer in the 1940s.

A moth became stuck in one of the archaic machine's large relays, and was damaging its functionality.

As she helped remove the moth, Admiral Hopper commented that they were "debugging" the machine.

However, this common myth is likely wrong; "debugging" was used in papers and media even in the 19th century.

It was commonly used in aeronautics engineering prior to the devleopment of computers.

But even Thomas Edison mentioned it in his writings, in the 1870s.

It might have emerged from very early mechanical engineering jargon.

Before circuitry and machines were miniaturized, real bugs could cause problems if they crept into machines.

But we don't know for sure when the word originated.

Rear Admiral Grace Hopper was an incredible engineer, though.

She earned a PhD in mathematics from Yale. She tried to join the Navy during World War II, but was rejected due not meeting physical requirements.

She instead joined the Navy Reserves and went through basic training before helping the Navy work on computers that were used in the Manhatten Project.

After the war, Admiral Hopper continued advancing computer science, working on the famous UNIVAC computer.

She developed one of the first compilers, a piece of software that we computer scientists love.

If we couldn't compile code, we couldn't write it in actual words and symbols. We'd need to use numbers.

It'd be bad.

So thank you, Admiral Hopper. You may not have originated a fun C.S. term, but you did save us all a lot of work.
